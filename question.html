Animating Question

What I want to understand

My pathway centers on how data becomes knowledge and authority, especially when richer information exists privately while public audiences still rely on simplified metrics.

How can computer vision and player-tracking data create more informative, transparent, and publicly accessible ways of evaluating hockey performance beyond current popular advanced metrics?

Why this matters

More data does not automatically mean better understanding

In hockey, public discussion often leans on a small set of visible advanced metrics such as xG share, GAR, or RAPM. Those numbers are useful, but they compress a complex game into a few outputs that can feel more definitive than they really are. Tracking data and computer vision create the possibility of measuring space, timing, access, and off-puck movement in ways that public event logs cannot, so my question is about how to use those richer signals without turning them into a new black box.

Pathway connection

Why this is a Data, Information & Society question

This is not only a technical modeling problem. It is also a question about interpretation, communication, unequal access to information, and the ethics of evidence-based claims. That fits the Pathway directly: who gets to make quantitative arguments, what kinds of data are visible to the public, and when models clarify knowledge versus when they create false certainty.

Three pieces of the question

The question is broad on purpose, but I currently see three linked parts inside it.

Part 1

Richer signals

I want to understand what tracking data and computer vision capture that event-only public metrics usually miss: spacing, pre-shot movement, passing lanes, support positioning, and context-dependent value.

Part 2

Transparent modeling

I care about models that are communicable. If a new metric cannot explain what it measures or why it matters, it risks becoming another opaque authority statistic.

Part 3

Public accessibility

I want this work to remain meaningful outside private team environments. That means thinking about tools, packages, and explanations that can make advanced analysis visible to broader audiences.

Current direction

Where the question is heading now

Right now the question is leaning toward an Ice Control Value framework: combining control, puck access, and continuation value to study zone-established offensive play in a way that is both technically rigorous and interpretable.
