Itinerary Courses

Coursework shaping the pathway

These three courses work together as a progression: mathematical foundations, machine learning methods, and then a future computer vision course that helps me better understand how visual and tracking-style data are created, processed, and limited before anyone starts modeling them.

Completed MAT 316 • Probability

Foundation for uncertainty, randomness, and model thinking

Connecticut College describes MAT 316 as a course on randomness and uncertainty covering conditional probability, random variables, distributions, expected value, variance, joint distributions, and the law of large numbers. This course matters for my pathway because nearly everything I want to do in analytics depends on probabilistic reasoning. It gave me the mathematical language behind uncertainty, prediction, and inference, which I need if I want my hockey models to be more than just computational experiments.

Official course description
Currently taking STA 336 • Statistical Machine Learning

From classical statistics to modern predictive methods

The official catalog describes STA 336 as an advanced course in important machine learning topics, including both supervised and unsupervised learning with applications in R. This is one of the clearest pathway courses for me because it directly supports the modeling side of my animating question. It helps me compare interpretable baselines with more flexible methods while keeping performance, validation, and communication in view at the same time.

Official course description
Planned COM 322 • Computer Vision

Seeing beyond event logs

Connecticut College describes COM 322 as a study of computational methods that enable machines to see, covering image formation, processing, feature detection, segmentation, recognition, and video processing. I have not taken this yet, but it is still central to the next phase of my question. More than collecting brand-new data myself, I want to understand the technical background behind how visual and tracking-style data are generated, what can go wrong in those pipelines, and what limits those methods impose on later analysis.

How these courses fit together

The value of the itinerary is in the sequence, not just the individual classes.

Probability gives the language

It grounds my understanding of uncertainty, expected outcomes, and what it means to make a defensible probabilistic claim.

Machine learning gives the modeling toolkit

It expands that foundation into prediction, validation, and comparison across different kinds of models and data.

Computer vision opens a new data layer

It helps me understand how richer visual and tracking-style signals are built, what their blind spots are, and how those upstream choices affect any downstream hockey model.
